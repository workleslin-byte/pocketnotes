02 — Overview

A 60-second
orientation.

A short orientation to the brand: what it is, who it is for, and what it refuses to do. Everything that follows expands on this page.

What we are

Pocket Notes is an India-based notebook brand. Two SKU lines — Founder's 500 and Flow. A6, 64 pages, staple-bound, faint grid on cream paper.

We are a capture device. Not a planner. Not a journal. Not stationery. The product is small on purpose; the constraint is the feature.

Who we're for

People who think in sparks. Founders who get ideas mid-meeting. Writers who hate perfect first pages. Songwriters catching a line in transit. Product folks scribbling flows on paper.

What we don't do

We don't build planner systems. We don't sell aesthetic affirmations. We don't make notebooks that feel "too good to spoil." We compete with the notes app, sticky notes, memory, and chaos.

03 — Positioning

Where we sit,
and against what.

Positioning is not a promise. It is a refusal. The clearest brands earn attention by saying out loud what they will not do.

Core positioning

This isn't stationery. This is a capture device for fast thinkers.

Canonical brand line

The four-line frame

A discipline borrowed from product strategy: a brand earns clarity when it can fill all four lines without softening any of them.

Forpeople who think in sparks — founders, writers, designers, songwriters, illustrators, product folks, night-scribblers.
Whoneed to catch ideas at the speed they appear, somewhere that doesn't argue with the moment.
Unlikethe notes app, sticky notes, memory, chaos — and unlike Moleskine, which sells the idea of writing.
Wemake small grid notebooks for people who already write, and want a tool that respects the unfinished state of a thought.

Five pillars

The underlying beliefs in the order they decide a question. When a design or copy choice gets stuck, walk down this list.

01

The pocket constraint is a gift

A small page forces choice. Every word earns its space. Brevity becomes a writing practice. We never widen the page.

02

Grid pages free the mind

Not lined. Not blank. Grid. It holds structure loosely — for words, sketches, lists that turn into poems, flows that turn into product.

03

Ancient notebooks were the internet

Roman wax tablets. Persian poetry diaries. Indian kavyagrantha. Knowledge lived in physical books carried on the body. Our category is older than typing.

04

Complexity is the enemy of writing

The best system is the one you actually use. No subscription. No battery. No syncing. Anywhere a feature creeps in — clasps, ribbons, undated weeklies — we cut it.

05

We compete with chaos, not Moleskine

Our true competitors are forgetting, the notes app you don't open, and the sticky note that fell behind the desk. Other notebook brands are a category — they aren't the fight.

Category placement

Most "notebook brands" sit inside stationery. We deliberately step out. We sit closer to tools-for-thinking — alongside Field Notes, Baron Fig, and the methods of writers like Robert Greene or Tiago Forte. The product happens to be a notebook. The category is capture.

04 — Voice & tone

How we sound
on a quiet day.

Voice is the shape of our sentences regardless of context. Tone shifts when the room shifts — a customer email is calmer than a launch caption. The voice does not change.

Five attributes

Direct

Subject, verb, point. We say what we mean in the order the reader needs it. The first sentence of every page is doing work by the time it ends.

Useful

Every sentence should feel like a tool. If a line teaches the reader nothing and gets nothing done, it gets cut. Borrowed from Ogilvy.

Intelligent

We assume our reader is smart, busy, and slightly skeptical. We never explain a word twice. No five-syllable word where one syllable lands harder.

Sparked

Reading our copy should make the reader want to write something. Momentum in, momentum out. The page is a starting gun, not a finish line.

Minimal

No filler adjectives. No throat-clearing. Tight paragraphs, hard line breaks, room around the words. As compressed as the product.

Four do/don't pairs

Don't

"Premium notebooks, thoughtfully designed for the modern creative."

Premium, thoughtfully designed, modern creative — three filler words in eight. Nothing being said.

Do

"Small grid notebooks, made in India, for people who already write."

Concrete: size, paper, country, audience. Reader knows in one sentence whether it's for them.

Don't

"Our mission is to empower thinkers to elevate their creative process."

Mission-statement voice. Empower, elevate, creative process — three abstractions stacked.

Do

"Catch the idea before it leaves the room."

A specific instruction the reader can follow today. Same belief, half the words.

Don't

"Discover your potential. Begin your journey. Manifest your dreams."

Instagram-affirmation voice. Potential, journey, manifest — vocabulary banned in the source brief.

Do

"Two lines. Every day. No matter what."

A ritual the reader could start in the next sixty seconds.

Don't

"Apologies for the delay! Your order is on its way and we hope you'll love it!"

Two exclamation marks, an apology that wasn't needed, hope-you'll-love-it that infantilises.

Do

"Your set is in transit. Tracking below. It will reach you in 3–5 working days."

Calm, factual, useful. The customer gets the information they came for.

3px stroke, every line

The stroke weight makes every illustration feel hand-drawn even when it's geometric. Never use 1px (too clinical) or 5px (too cartoon).

08 — Motion

Weighted.
Never zippy.

Motion is felt in this brand. It is intentional, weighted, calm. It says this object has mass. It never feels eager, springy, or polite. Two easing curves do almost all the work.

--ease

The default. A controlled exhale. Used for hover transitions, state changes, anything routine.

cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)

--spring

Used sparingly. For magnetic CTAs, hover-fold corners, anywhere a small overshoot feels playful but never silly.

cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1)

Four motion principles

Slow in, faster out

Most transitions take 350–500ms. Never under 200ms — that reads as nervous. Never over 800ms — that reads as broken.

One thing at a time

If the corner radius is changing, the transform is also changing — together, in the same easing. Never staggered, never independent.

Pin-and-fold for hero scrolls

The main site uses a clip-path curtain reveal between hero panels. Reserved for moments that earn it. Never on body content.

Respect reduced-motion

Every animated CSS rule has a prefers-reduced-motion override. Marquees freeze. Spin stops. Pin-stack collapses to natural scroll.

12 — Do & don't

Eighteen rules
worth printing.

Each rule pairs a refusal with the move that earns it. The "why" line is the part most brand books leave out — without it, the rule rots the moment a fresh hand picks up the work.

Don'tDo

"Premium notebooks for the discerning creative."

Small grid notebooks, made in India, for people who already write.

Why · Premium and discerning are stationery-shop voice. Concrete > aspirational, every time.

Don'tDo

Bleached white office paper, blue lines.

Cream paper, faint grid. Always.

Why · Cream is half our brand. White paper makes us look like a printer's afterthought.

Don'tDo

Inter or DM Sans for headlines.

Fraunces, weight 900, with one italic word in coral.

Why · Geometric sans for headlines makes the brand feel like a SaaS landing page. Fraunces is the personality.

Don'tDo

Coral as a body-text color.

Coral for italics, links, accent words only — usually inside a Fraunces <em>.

Why · Coral on cream falls under WCAG AA at body sizes. Saving it for accents also keeps it from feeling cheap.

Don'tDo

Gradients on backgrounds, buttons, illustrations.

Solid color blocks. Surface shifts (cream → bone → paper) for depth.

Why · The brand is hand-drawn warmth, not Photoshop. Gradients read as 2014 startup.

Don'tDo

Drop shadows on cards.

1.5px ink borders + a surface-color shift.

Why · Shadows soften everything. Borders keep things flat, declarative, printerly.

Don'tDo

Lucide / Heroicons / generic icon sets.

Inline SVG illustrations drawn at 3px stroke, with a face if it's an object.

Why · Generic icons are tone-flat. Our illustrations are part of the voice.

Don'tDo

Stock photography of hands holding notebooks.

Inline SVG, or a real product photo on a single warm-cream backdrop.

Why · Stock photos make the brand look like every other notebook brand. They erase the only thing that makes us specific.

Don'tDo

Arbitrary spacing values.

Multiples of 4 or 8px. Section padding 5vw. Max width 1280px.

Why · Discipline at the system level shows up in print. Arbitrary spacing reads as amateur.

Don'tDo

Animations under 200ms or over 800ms.

350–500ms with the --ease curve. Reserve --spring for magnetic moments.

Why · Under 200ms reads nervous. Over 800ms reads broken. The middle is where weight is felt.

Don'tDo

Two exclamation marks anywhere. Ever.

One exclamation mark per page, used like a knife.

Why · The voice is calm. Multiple exclamations break the entire register.

Don'tDo

Emoji in marketing copy.

Inline SVG stars, dots, arrows — drawn in the system.

Why · Emoji change rendering across devices and read as Instagram-brand. Our chrome is hand-drawn.

Don'tDo

Foreign currencies as the default.

India-first. The local currency leads. International is exception, not default.

Why · We are an Indian brand. The currency is the first signal of who we are speaking to.

Don'tDo

"Your journey starts here."

"Begin a notebook this week."

Why · Journey is one of the brand's banned words — abstract, lifestyle-coach voice. Specific instruction beats abstract noun.

Don'tDo

Long bulleted feature lists on product pages.

One sentence describing what the product does. Three concrete specs in mono. One italic line at the end.

Why · Bulleted features sound like Amazon. Sentences sound like a person.

Don'tDo

"Crafted with love."

"Made by hand. Made in India."

Why · "Crafted with love" is the cliché the brand was created to avoid. Specific verbs do the same emotional work without the cringe.

Don'tDo

Apologetic shipping copy ("Sorry for any inconvenience").

"Your set ships tomorrow morning. Tracking will follow."

Why · Apologise only when you actually wronged someone. Default-apologising erodes the brand's calm authority.

Don'tDo

"Made with ink & intention."

"Made with ink, shipped from India."

Why · "Ink & intention" is the kind of stationery-shop phrase the brand was created to avoid. Specific verbs do the same emotional work without the cringe.

13 — Closing

Carry the
method.

A brand book is a way to remember a decision without re-making it every Tuesday. Print this. Pin the rules page near your desk. Hand it to the next designer on day one. When in doubt, return to the overview.

Small books, big ideas.
Carry one. Begin the work.
