The First Page Rule

The notebook you're saving for your best ideas will stay empty. The rule is simple: ruin the first page immediately. Write something bad on it. What you unlock is everything that comes after.

There is a kind of paralysis that arrives with a new notebook. The pages are clean. The binding is stiff. There is a strong suggestion, emanating from the object itself, that whatever goes in first should be good. Worthy. Considered. And so the notebook sits. A week passes. Two weeks. The clean pages reproach gently every time you walk past. Eventually you stop noticing them altogether.

This is not laziness. It is a design problem. The pristine notebook has created a standard no first thought can meet, and so the notebook functions as an object of admiration rather than a tool. It is the writing equivalent of the good china — preserved for occasions that never quite arrive.

The fix is deliberate and permanent. Open to the first page. Write something bad on it. Not bad as in careless — bad as in genuinely, willingly imperfect. Your grocery list. Today's date and the first four words that cross your mind. A bad drawing of whatever is in front of you. The point is that the first page is now ruined. The standard has been broken. There is nothing left to protect.

"You can't think yourself out of a writing block; you have to write yourself out of a thinking block."

— John Rogers

What happens after the first page is freed is not that the quality improves immediately. It is that the anxiety lifts. The notebook is no longer a vault for your best ideas. It is a working surface. Ideas that are rough and half-formed can go in — which is to say, all ideas, since all ideas start rough and half-formed. You stop filtering at the input stage, which is the only stage where filtering actively damages the work.

Some writers go further. They keep two notebooks: one for working in, one as a backup in case the working one is lost or destroyed. The backup never gets opened. The working one looks like it's been through a campaign. Pages dog-eared, ink varying in pressure, things crossed out, a phone number that means nothing, a sketch that isn't finished. It has the texture of a tool that has been used. The backup has the texture of an object that has been owned.

The Founder's 500 is a working notebook. It is not designed to stay clean. The grid accepts anything: text, diagrams, crossed-out lines, the corner doodle that has nothing to do with anything. The thick cover will mark and dent with use. This is not a defect. It is evidence.

A notebook that shows wear is a notebook that has been used. A notebook used is a notebook that has done its job. That is the whole purpose of the object: to receive thought without conditions.

Open it. Write something bad. Now you can begin.

— Pocket Notes, May 2026
